# Mercer-Karhunen-Loeve Expansion

**Original:** [stats/MercerKarhunenLoeve](https://www.chebfun.org/examples/stats/MercerKarhunenLoeve.html)
**Author(s):** Toby Driscoll, December 2011

Mercer's theorem is a continuous analog of the eigenvalue decomposition of a
symmetric positive definite matrix. One of its main applications is to find
convenient representations of stochastic processes via the Karhunen--Loeve (KL)
expansion [1].

## Mercer's theorem

Suppose $K(s,t)$ is a symmetric, continuous, and nonnegative definite kernel on
$[a,b] \times [a,b]$. Mercer's theorem asserts that there exist orthonormal
eigenfunctions $\psi_j(x)$ and eigenvalues $\lambda_j$ such that

$$K(s,t) = \sum_{j=1}^{\infty} \lambda_j\,\psi_j(s)\,\psi_j(t),$$

where the eigenvalues and eigenfunctions satisfy the integral eigenvalue equation

$$\lambda_j\,\psi_j(s) = \int_a^b K(s,t)\,\psi_j(t)\,dt.$$

For the exponential kernel $K(s,t) = e^{-|s-t|}$, the leading eigenmodes can be
computed numerically using a Fredholm integral operator. The eigenfunctions are
orthonormal, but truncating the Mercer sum underestimates $K(s,s)$. The
eigenvalues decrease like $O(n^{-2})$, making pointwise convergence slow.

## Karhunen-Loeve expansion

If $X(t,\omega)$ is a stochastic process with mean $\mu(t)$ and covariance
$K(s,t) = E[(X(s) - \mu(s))(X(t) - \mu(t))]$, the KL expansion expresses

$$X(t,\omega) = \mu(t) + \sum_{j=1}^{\infty} \sqrt{\lambda_j}\,\psi_j(t)\,Z_j(\omega),$$

where $Z_j$ are uncorrelated random variables with unit variance. This is a
generalization of the SVD: the covariance $K$ plays the role of the Gram matrix,
and truncation at the largest eigenvalues gives the best approximation.

Because the $Z_j$ are uncorrelated, the total variance of $X$ is the sum of
eigenvalues, which equals $\int_a^b K(s,s)\,ds$. For the exponential kernel on
$[-1,1]$, this is 2, and 95% of the variance is captured by the first 10 KL
modes.

Random realizations of the process can be generated by sampling the $Z_j$ as
i.i.d. standard normals and summing the truncated expansion. The sample
covariance of many realizations recovers the original kernel $K(s,t)$.

Shortening the correlation length (increasing the rate of exponential decay)
means more randomness, and a larger number of modes is needed to capture the
same fraction of the variance.
